# Limitations Note

Main limitations:

- The study used fixed hypothetical scenarios rather than a randomized live-chatbot experiment.
- The scenarios differed in total errors, error rate, timing, consistency, wording, and fixed order, so H2 cannot isolate inconsistency as a causal factor.
- Attention was self-reported rather than observed through reading time, source checks, clicks, or eye tracking.
- The attention item was asymmetric: one broad more-carefully option, two levels of less-careful reading, and no slightly/much-more distinction.
- Switching and giving up were measured as intended first actions rather than observed behavior.
- The grouped H4 outcome combines external search, switching AI tools, and abandonment even though these may represent different processes.
- The five-item exploratory composite combines affect, effort, confidence, time appraisal, and giving-up intention; some item content overlaps the H3/H4 outcomes.
- The sample was a nonprobability convenience sample and may not represent all AI chatbot users.
- Platform use allowed multiple selections, so its percentages can sum above 100%.
- The exact-content duplicate rule was documented after collection; the supplied materials did not include preregistered exclusions or attention checks.
- The 181 retained records are response-content patterns, not verified unique identities.
- Twenty-four retained records selected "Under 18," but the supplied materials did not document the applicable consent/assent, parental-permission, or ethics determination.
- The 37 optional written responses lacked a prespecified codebook, coder information, reflexivity statement, or documented theme-development procedure. They are illustrative comments, not a formal thematic analysis.

These limitations mean the verification shift should be presented as a conceptual interpretation and future hypothesis, not as a causal mechanism demonstrated by this survey.
